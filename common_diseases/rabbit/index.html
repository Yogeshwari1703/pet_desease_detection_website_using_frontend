Rabbit Disease Encyclopedia

Comprehensive guide to common rabbit illnesses and treatments

Respiratory Tract Infections (Snuffles) Severe
Rabbit

Overview

Upper respiratory infection often caused by Pasteurella multocida or Moraxella catarrhalis. Can become chronic and spread to other organs.

Symptoms

  • Sneezing, nasal discharge (mucus/pus)
  • Eye discharge, redness, squinting
  • Crusty fur on paws from rubbing
  • Head tilt (if ears infected)
  • Abscesses, pneumonia, or sudden death in severe cases

Treatment

Long-term antibiotics (months), surgical drainage of abscesses. Rarely fully cured.

Encephalitozoon cuniculi Severe
Rabbit

Overview

Microsporidian parasite affecting brain, eyes, kidneys, and GI tract. Causes permanent damage.

Symptoms

  • Head tilt, rolling, imbalance
  • White fluffy material in eye (permanent)
  • Kidney failure (weight loss, dehydration)
  • Recurring GI stasis

Treatment

Fenbendazole (anti-parasitic), supportive care. Early treatment critical.

Gastrointestinal (GI) Stasis Critical
Rabbit

Overview

Life-threatening slowdown of gut motility due to stress, dental disease, or infections.

Symptoms

  • No appetite, small/no fecal pellets
  • Lethargy, hunched posture
  • Bloating, loud gut sounds (or silence)
  • Tooth grinding (pain)

Treatment

Emergency vet care: fluids, syringe feeding, pain relief, motility drugs (e.g., cisapride).

Tyzzer Disease Critical
Rabbit

Overview

Caused by Clostridium piliforme. Damages intestines, liver, and heart. 90% mortality in outbreaks.

Symptoms

  • Watery diarrhea, dehydration
  • Lethargy, stained hindquarters
  • Death within 1–3 days (acute cases)
  • Wasting disease in chronic cases

Treatment

Oxytetracycline may help. Disinfect with 1% peracetic acid or 3% bleach. Often fatal.

Rabbit Hemorrhagic Disease (RHDV2) Critical
Rabbit

Overview

Highly contagious calicivirus causing liver damage and internal bleeding. 60–90% mortality.

Symptoms

  • Sudden death (often no prior signs)
  • Fever, lethargy before death
  • Liver failure, hemorrhaging

Treatment

No cure. Prevention: Vaccination (2-dose series + boosters) and strict biosecurity.

Treponematosis (Rabbit Syphilis) Moderate
Rabbit

Overview

Venereal disease caused by Treponema bacteria. Spread via mating or mother-to-offspring.

Symptoms

  • Blisters/scabs on genitals, lips, eyelids
  • Slow-healing sores

Treatment

Penicillin injections (never oral). Heals in 10–14 days. Isolate infected rabbits.

Moraxella catarrhalis Infection Moderate
Rabbit

Overview

Respiratory pathogen often co-occurring with other infections. Linked to stress and poor hygiene.

Symptoms

  • Diarrhea, bloating
  • Loss of appetite, weight loss
  • Eye/nasal discharge (if respiratory)

Treatment

Antibiotics (vet-supervised), stress reduction, and improved sanitation.

Epizootic Rabbit Enteropathy Severe
Rabbit

Overview

Highly contagious bacterial disease (unknown pathogen) causing severe GI symptoms.

Symptoms

  • Watery diarrhea, mucous excretion
  • Distended abdomen, rumbling gut
  • Cecal impaction, high mortality

Treatment

Supportive care, isolate infected rabbits. No specific treatment known.

Colibacillosis (E. coli) Severe
Rabbit

Overview

E. coli-induced diarrhea, often fatal in young rabbits.

Symptoms

  • Yellowish diarrhea (fatal in 1–2-week-olds)
  • Weight loss, stunted growth (weaned kits)

Treatment

Antibiotics in mild cases. Sanitize environment. High-fiber diets may prevent.

Ringworm Mild
Rabbit

Overview

Fungal infection (Trichophyton mentagrophytes) spread by direct contact. Zoonotic.

Symptoms

  • Patchy hair loss (head first)
  • Dry, flaky, red skin (non-itchy)

Treatment

Antifungal creams/oral meds. Isolate rabbit; wear gloves when handling.
