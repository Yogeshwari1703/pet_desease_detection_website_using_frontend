Turtle Disease Encyclopedia

Comprehensive guide to common turtle illnesses and treatments

Nutritional Diseases Moderate
Turtle

Overview

Nutritional diseases of turtles have become less common due to better husbandry information and commercially available turtle diets. "Soft shell" (nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism) is caused by inadequate dietary calcium and vitamin D, excessive phosphorus, and lack of UV light.

Symptoms

  • Soft shell
  • Broken bones
  • Weakness
  • Swollen eyes (vitamin A deficiency)

Treatment

Early cases are reversible with diet correction, high-calcium foods, calcium and vitamin D3 supplements, and proper UV lighting. Vitamin A deficiency is treated with prescription vitamin A. Natural sunlight is ideal when possible.

Infectious Diseases Severe
Turtle

Overview

Infections are common in aquatic turtles and can affect the shell, respiratory tract, skin, ears, and digestive tract. In severe cases, infection can spread through the entire body via the circulatory system.

Symptoms

  • Shell discoloration
  • Bleeding or soft spots on shell
  • Abnormal keratin sloughing
  • Bad odor
  • Respiratory distress

Treatment

Treatment depends on the type and severity of infection. Shell infections range from mild topical treatments to severe cases requiring surgery for osteomyelitis (bone infection). Antibiotics are typically required.

Reproductive Diseases Severe
Turtle

Overview

Female turtles can develop reproductive issues including follicular stasis (failed ovulation), yolk coelomitis (leaking yolk material), and egg retention. Males may experience penis prolapse.

Symptoms

  • Lethargy
  • Loss of appetite
  • Straining
  • Visible prolapse (in males)
  • Abnormal swimming

Treatment

Many cases can be managed medically or surgically if detected early. Minimally invasive surgery techniques can remove retained eggs or diseased ovaries. Spaying female turtles can prevent future problems.

Vitamin A Deficiency Moderate
Turtle

Overview

Hypovitaminosis A occurs when turtles are fed an inappropriate diet (iceberg lettuce, all-meat diet, or poor-quality commercial diet). It affects skin, mucus-producing glands, and membranes.

Symptoms

  • Swollen eyelids (often with discharge)
  • Ear abscesses
  • Chronic respiratory infections
  • Lack of appetite
  • Lethargy
  • Kidney failure

Treatment

Treatment involves correcting the diet and vitamin A supplementation by prescription. Respiratory infections secondary to vitamin A deficiency may require additional antibiotic treatment.

Shell Infections Severe
Turtle

Overview

Shell rot is caused by bacteria, fungi, or parasites, often secondary to trauma, burns, or bites. Infections can penetrate deep into shell layers, causing ulcers, pitting, or bone infection.

Symptoms

  • Discolored shell
  • Pitting or ulcers on shell
  • Soft spots
  • Foul odor
  • Bleeding

Treatment

Treatment depends on severity, ranging from topical treatments for mild cases to surgical debridement and systemic antibiotics for severe osteomyelitis. Proper husbandry is crucial for prevention.

Gastrointestinal Parasites Moderate
Turtle

Overview

Roundworms and protozoa are common in pet turtles. Many infections don't cause clinical signs but can lead to serious problems in severe cases or immunocompromised turtles.

Symptoms

  • Diarrhea
  • Weight loss
  • Lethargy
  • Poor appetite
  • Visible worms in feces

Treatment

Diagnosed through fecal examination. Treatment depends on parasite type but may include dewormers or antiprotozoal medications. Severe cases may require supportive care like fluids and nutritional support.

Intestinal Obstruction Severe
Turtle

Overview

Occurs when there's a blockage in the digestive system, most often from eating gravel at the bottom of their tank. The behavior may be natural for digestion, mineral acquisition, or due to boredom/hunger.

Symptoms

  • Anorexia
  • Lack of defecation
  • Difficulty swimming
  • Lethargy
  • Abdominal distension

Treatment

Diagnosed through X-rays. Treatment depends on obstruction location and size - may include inducing vomiting or surgical removal. Prevention involves using appropriate substrate and providing proper nutrition.

Shell Fracture Critical
Turtle

Overview

A very serious condition resulting from blunt force trauma that cracks the shell or causes pieces to come off. The shell protects internal organs, so fractures can be life-threatening.

Symptoms

  • Cracked or missing shell pieces
  • Visible injury
  • Pain response
  • Bleeding
  • Lethargy

Treatment

Requires immediate veterinary care. X-rays determine severity. Treatment may involve medical-grade plastics or epoxy cement to repair shell. Severe cases may need surgical intervention. Proper aftercare is crucial for recovery.

Cystic Calculi Severe
Turtle

Overview

Bladder stones occur when minerals from the diet form crystals in urine that stick together. Usually results from improper nutrition and/or dehydration. The turtle's shell makes surgical treatment challenging.

Symptoms

  • Blood in droppings
  • Straining to defecate
  • Grunting during bowel movements
  • Lethargy
  • Loss of appetite

Treatment

Diagnosed through physical exam and X-rays. Stone removal generally requires surgery (either through shell or pelvic approach) plus fluid therapy. Dietary and environmental modifications help prevent recurrence.

Tissue Prolapses Critical
Turtle

Overview

Occurs when an organ (intestine, cloaca, bladder, uterus, or penis) protrudes from the vent. In males, temporary penis prolapse during stress or mating is normal if it retracts easily. Prolapsed tissue is life-threatening if not treated.

Symptoms

  • Visible tissue protruding from vent
  • Swelling
  • Straining
  • Lethargy
  • Bleeding

Treatment

Requires immediate veterinary attention. Exposed tissue can dry out, suffer blood flow compromise, or be bitten by tank mates. Treatment may involve reduction, lubrication, sutures, or surgery depending on severity and tissue involved.
